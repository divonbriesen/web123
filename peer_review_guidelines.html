Peer Review Guidelines

We analyzed every peer review written in this course — over 800 of them. The median review is 30 words. Some 17-word reviews are excellent, and some 40-worders are empty cheerleading. Word count is the wrong axis. What follows is what actually separates them, with real examples from your classmates (names changed).

What Good Reviews Do

  • Report something they tested, not something they saw. "I submitted my name and favorite game on your Who page, and nothing happened on submission" (Greg). "WAVE gave you 4.9/10 with contrast errors" (Sean). "The Validate HTML link points to the top of the page instead of the validator" (Suman). The reviewer exercised the site — clicked, submitted, ran a validator.
  • Name the exact location and symptom. "The design firm link goes to ../yourfirm.html instead of ../yourfirm.com" (Jordan) — findable and fixable in one minute.
  • Tie the finding to the instructions or standards. Tyler quoted the figure rule back verbatim — "each section should include at least one figure (image + caption + italicized paragraph explaining the AI prompt used)" — and then suggested how to fix it ("perhaps a small font so it doesn't disrupt your design"). Tariq cited the no-black/white rule and offered the justify-it-in-a-comment alternative. These are the best reviews in the course.
  • Say what passes as specifically as what fails. "No accumulus errors, no HTML or CSS errors, accessibility 10/10" is a verifiable claim — praise that proves work was done.
  • Engage with other reviews. "Like Evan, I also get a 404 — make sure your repository is public" (Kara) — confirming a peer's finding and adding the fix.

What Bad Reviews Do

  • Verdict without evidence. "Everything looks great! You followed the instructions perfectly!" — 8 words or 80, there's no sign anything was opened.
  • Template reuse. The same "Your website looks pretty nice. Missing/Things to improve:" shell pasted across targets; one student pasted the same paragraph twice inside one review; several mutual "you followed all instructions, everything is satisfied" pairs. Reused text counts as zero.
  • Cheerleading as content. "Keep up the momentum! The next modules are going to be a lot of fun!" — encouragement is nice but it reviews nothing.
  • Risky unchecked praise. "Crystal clear screenshot, no mistake at all" — on a submission that had real issues. False all-clears are worse than silence, because the author stops fixing.

The Guideline

A review must contain, in any length:

  • One tested observation — something you clicked, submitted, validated, or measured, with the result.
  • One specific finding or fix, named by location (page/element/link), or an explicit pass-list of what you verified.
  • A tie to the instructions or standards where relevant — quote or cite the rule.
  • In your own words — reused text across reviews counts as zero.

Reviews are automatically screened on these axes (specifics named, evidence of testing, text reuse) — not to grade you by machine, but to decide which reviews get a closer human look.

The Format (every peer review, on every assignment that requires peer reviews)

Start with their @name — first and last, like @Emily Sanchez. Type @ and pick their name from the popup that appears (it may also complete automatically) — a real Canvas tag notifies them; typing the plain characters does not.

Cut-and-paste template

@Firstname Lastname
- FIRST LOOK:
- STOP:
- START:
- CONTINUE:

A complete example (about seventy words)

  • @Emily Sanchez
  • FIRST LOOK: whoa, the header photo is striking. Took me a second to find the nav though. — your honest gut reaction, written the moment the page opens, before you check anything; the one slot allowed to be pure reaction.
  • STOP: linking your firm with an absolute URL: use ../mametzdesigns.com/ per the standards. — something that violates the standards or hurts the site, named by location.
  • START: adding the italic prompt note under each figure (instructions require image + caption + prompt). — something missing that the instructions require; quote or cite the rule.
  • CONTINUE: the component header/footer: I checked three pages and it's consistent everywhere. — something working, with what you checked to know it works; praise that proves work was done.

A labeled "START: add more content" with no location or rule is still an empty review. Nothing genuinely wrong to report? Say what you tested in CONTINUE and use STOP/START for the closest thing to an improvement you can find — there is always one.
